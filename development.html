Understanding Context and Audience

The atlas is intended for an audience of people hiking Costa Rica’s network of trails. In its early stages of development, the project will be limited to a certain audience by the technology and language used. With continued development the project is intended to serve the complete audience of users for Costa Rica’s trail networks.

In order to serve the intended community in the best way possible, it was decided that the initial atlas product would be developed primarily for a spanish speaking community. Trail users are presently predominantly spanish speaking and as such, this app will serve them well. This choice of language also serves the local spanish speaking communities along the trails. The use of spanish in early product development allows local community members to easily engage with the project, understand, and contribute. We hope this relationship will remain strong as the Atlas is adapted to alternative languages in future development.

Though the atlas is intended for a wider audience of the Costa Rican hiking community, the project’s initial contact with this audience to the form the La Alianza for el Sendero Pacifico (The Alliance for the Pacific Trail) and Los Senderos de Costa Rica (The Trails of Costa Rica). These are informal alliances of community members, businesses, and organizations directed at supporting their regional or national trail networks. This audience contributed valuable data, testing, and feedback during this current phase of development. Both groups have expressed interest in continuing their involvement in the project. This collaboration is critical to the project’s success as it allows local stakeholders to directly participate in its development.

Project Initiation

Prior to this project, starting in 2017 the WTN Hub for the Americas, in conjunction with La Alianza por el Sendero Pacifico, has engaged students from various Universities including Yale, Worcester Polytechnic Institute, and Middlebury College in work related to geographic information products related to the region surround El Sendero Pacifico. These efforts resulted in valuable data that has been used to study regional conservation efforts and develop maps to support regional trail based recreation. Related work had been completed at the Monteverde Institute starting as early as 2004. This project is a continuation of these efforts to support the area with actionable geographic information.

The collaboration between Clark University and the World Trails Network arose from a chance meeting between mutual friends in the White Mountains of New Hampshire. An initial discussion revealed the desire for, among other products, an online map detailing the trail network of El Sendero Pacifico, which would include digital media such as pictures, video, and web links, along with details of the trails and adjacent communities. This product had the potential to be coupled with a parallel project being developed by students at Worcester Polytechnic Institute. This parallel project was focused on supporting businesses in the trail-adjacent communities in a move to e-commerce, in order to expand their reach regionally and potentially internationally. Following this introduction and discussion of concepts, a brief onboarding period ensued during which a formal working relationship was formed and resources including data and collaboration platforms were shared.

Data Development

Prior to developing the desired cartographic applications, the underlying data that would describe the trail network needed to be developed. That this data already existed to a great extent was a great boon to the pace of the project. An earlier project that the World Trails Network had been engaged in with students from Worcester State, had developed a database containing a wide range of vector data related to local administration, utilities, natural features, and trail networks. The data most pertinent to the project, the trail networks, was collected by students and community members in years prior to the start of this project. Additional geographic data regarding the trails were collected from publicly available sources including Wikiloc.

The existing trail data was available in mixed forms including both raw field data and carefully edited data, but in all cases were not entirely complete. These data were aggregated and edited in ArcGIS pro. Preparing these data for editing and later use included the following:

  1. converting data to Esri’s proprietary Shapefile format,
  2. converting raw ordered points from GPS data to lines,
  3. simplifying line geometry to remove repeated segments, artefacts of gps inaccuracy, and non-trail segments,
  4. merging and splitting lines to produce logical segments between trail intersections, and
  5. calculating line segment lengths.

Following this primary editing stage a more collaborative process began between the student and stakeholders familiar with the trails. This process spanned a number of weeks and involved interactive sessions held over video conference where information regarding trail surface type, recommended mode of travel, access restrictions, colloquial names, and other details were added to the map.

Additional contextual data was added alongside the trail data throughout development. This data includes communities along El Sendero Pacifico, local high points, important reference points along the trail, attractions and dangers along the trail, and relevant commercial establishments. Much of this contextual data was produced by the past work of students and communities, mentioned earlier, or in the collaborative process described above. In the case of information related to products available in each community along El Sendero Pacifico, the data were collected in a process led by WPI students in parallel to this project. Their process involved informational interviews held over video conference with businesses and community members in the immediate vicinity of El Sendero Pacifico.

Beyond geographic data and their attributes, related visual and electronic media were gathered to provide a more engaging experience for atlas users. These media include photos embedded in informational popups attached to trails, communities, and other locations. These also include hyperlinks to other resources online and embedded videos, currently hosted on youtube, that had been produced by a previous project driven by La Alianza por el Sendero Pacifico.

Interface Development

In its present state, the atlas is implemented in Esri’s ArcGIS Web AppBuilder. This powerful web mapping tool allowed for rapid, low-code development and a simple transition of data from desktop editing software (Esri’s ArcGIS Pro) to a publicly accessible interface. Prior to the present interface, a number of prototypes were developed using a range of programmable graphical libraries. These prototypes used services including Mapbox GL JS, Leaflet, and ArcGIS API for Javascript. These intermediary product served as both demonstrations of the services and means of reviewing the data that had been developed. Based on observations during the interface development phase, the trail and ancillary data were continuously updated to correct any mistakes or add new data.

Esri’s ArcGIS Web AppBuilder was ultimately chosen for full interface development due to it’s ease of use, wide range of functionality, and interoperability with the data editing platform used in data development. Transitioning from the data development process to interface development was a simple and smooth process enabled by Portal for ArcGIS Pro. This tool in ArcGIS Pro allows for quick data migration between the desktop software and ArcGIS Online. Once uploaded to ArcGIS Online, the data can be displayed, styled,and modified using a wide range of online tools. If changes are made to the data using desktop applications, the updated data can be easily uploaded to ArcGIS Online, either overwriting the existing files or creating ones. This easy interoperability was key to making it easy to manage the parallel data editing and interface development phases.

With the trail and ancillary data uploaded to ArcGIS Online, ArcGIS Map Viewer Classic was used to display and style these in a single map. Using Map Viewer Classic informational popup windows were formatted for features in the map, display settings were adjusted including limiting visibility at different zoom levels, and features were styled in a way to enhance user experience. This is also where initial map extent was set and legend elements were formatted.

Once the map was composed using Map Viewer Classic, it was added to an ArcGIS Web AppBuilder instance. This software allows for development of the interface that surrounds the map data using a graphical interface. Though no coding is necessary to use this product, there are opportunities to further customize elements within it using web development languages (Hyper Text Markup Language, Cascading Style Sheets, and Javascript). In this stage of development various elements in the interface were added and formatted, including the layer list, legend, informational panel, basemap gallery, as well as navigational tools.

Throughout the development process, supplemental information intended for the interface and map elements were translated to colloquial Costa Rican Spanish. This was necessary to ensure the resulting product would be understood by the primary audience at this stage of the project. Though the product is intended to eventually be accessible to a wider audience of non-Spanish language speakers, this initial development values local community engagement more highly than global accessibility. A number of community members contributed to the translations that have been used in the present version of the Atlas.

Basemap Design

Due to the focused nature of this web map, a custom basemap was necessary to minimize distraction from the map’s intended content and maximize relevant ancillary content. This basemap was developed using Mapbox Studio. This web-based software comes readily available with basemap templates and a wealth of global data layers including points of interest, terrain models, road lines, building footprints, administrative boundaries, land cover and use types, and satellite imagery. Using the graphical interface each of these layers display and style properties can be customized as well as adding or removing layers. In this way, a number of adjustments were made to the existing “Outdoors” basemap template to produce the custom basemap currently on display in the atlas.

  • Points of Interest: In order to avoid conflict with points of interest submitted by community members the existing layer depicting points of interest.
  • Topographic Contour lines: The intervals between topographic contour lines were reduced to increase visibility in Costa Rica’s limited altitudinal variation.
  • Country Mask: To focus attention on the area of interest, a semi-opaque mask was added to all countries other than Costa Rica.
  • National Protected Areas: The existing layer of protected areas was removed and replaced with a modified version that accounted for recent changes reported by SINAC (National System of Conservation Areas)
  • Reforestation Areas: Areas currently under private conservation and reforestation within the region of El Sendero Pacifico was developed from WTN data layers and added to the basemap.
  • Municipalities: With the intention of focusing attention on municipalities related to the trails displayed, the existing layer of municipal areas was removed. Points of municipalities were added to the map in ArcGIS instead of via the basemap in Mapbox Studio.
  • Trails: Though the basemap template contains a very detailed layer of trails and paths, many of which are depicted in the Atlas, they are not required for the purposes of our basemap. The existing trails layer was removed from the basemap.
